When Meditation Makes Things Worse

Most mindfulness instruction begins with a gentle disclaimer: "If anything feels uncomfortable, you can open your eyes, shift your posture, take a break." What it rarely says is: for a meaningful subset of people, discomfort isn't a temporary adjustment phase. It's a signal that the practice is producing the wrong thing.

This isn't a fringe concern. A 2019 study by Willoughby Britton and colleagues found that 58% of meditators in a survey reported at least one adverse effect — altered sensory perception, anxiety, derealization, emotional blunting, or involuntary movements. A quarter described effects that were "moderately distressing or worse." These weren't beginner mistakes. Many were experienced practitioners.

The research landscape

The evidence for meditation's benefits is real but narrower than popular accounts suggest. The strongest effects are modest reductions in anxiety and depression in clinical populations. The effect sizes look small next to the cultural footprint — but they're real, and for some people, the effects are substantial.

What gets less coverage is the adverse events literature. Britton's work at Brown — the Cheetah House project — has been documenting adverse meditation experiences since 2010. Their taxonomy includes:

  • Affective instability: intense emotions, mood swings, emotional anesthesia
  • Cognitive and perceptual changes: derealization, depersonalization, hypersensitivity to light or sound
  • Somatic changes: involuntary movements, increased pain, insomnia
  • Changes in sense of self: dissolution, fragmentation, ego inflation

Most of these are not cause for alarm in the short term — some are recognized stages in contemplative traditions. The concern is when they persist, intensify, or significantly impair functioning.

Who is at risk

Risk factors are not always what you'd expect. Trauma history is the most documented — not because meditation causes trauma, but because concentrated inward attention can surface material that was successfully submerged. For people with unprocessed traumatic memories, formal sitting practice can become a controlled destabilization.

Dissociative tendencies are another flag. Meditation, particularly open monitoring styles, trains a kind of decentering from experience. In someone already prone to dissociation, this can amplify rather than modulate the pattern.

Bipolar spectrum conditions present specific risks with intensive practice. There are documented cases of practice-induced hypomanic states, particularly in retreat settings.

A note on context: This isn't an argument against meditation. Most people benefit, and the benefits are well-documented. This is about the people who don't — and the fact that the field has been slow to discuss them honestly.

The mechanisms

Why would a practice oriented toward calming and clarity produce anxiety, dissociation, or emotional flooding? A few mechanisms are plausible:

Reduced suppression. A lot of what keeps daily functioning stable is a diffuse, mostly unconscious suppression of difficult material. Meditation — especially when practiced intensively — can weaken this. Content surfaces not because the practice summoned it, but because the usual containment is temporarily offline.

Heightened interoception. Mindfulness training consistently increases awareness of internal body states. For people with health anxiety, PTSD, or somatic symptoms, this can amplify rather than reduce distress. You're now more aware of the tight chest, not less.

Dissolution of the narrative self. Much of the ego's defensive function involves maintaining a coherent story about who you are and why things are okay. Meditation can interrupt this. For people in stable states, this is the interesting bit — the moment of seeing through the construction. For people in unstable states, losing the narrative can feel like losing the floor.

What teachers often miss

The mainstream mindfulness world has a difficult relationship with adverse effects. A few dynamics produce systematic under-reporting:

The attribution problem. When something difficult happens during practice, the framing is often "this is your resistance" or "the practice is working." This isn't always wrong, but it's an unfalsifiable explanation that makes it hard for a practitioner to identify when something is actually going wrong.

The MBSR completion bias. Most of the positive evidence base comes from 8-week programs, which preferentially retain people who are responding well. Dropouts don't appear in the outcomes data.

Insufficient intake screening. Secular mindfulness programs — corporate wellness, apps, MBSR — rarely conduct the kind of intake screening that would flag trauma history, dissociative tendencies, or active mental health concerns. Teachers are often not trained to recognize adverse effects in progress.

What to do

If you're experiencing persistent distress, increasing dissociation, or a significant worsening of mood or anxiety during or after meditation:

  • Reduce session length, or stop formal sitting temporarily. "Push through" is not always the right instruction.
  • Consider a different anchor. Open monitoring of breath and body sensations is not the only approach. Some people do better with a focus object outside the body — a candle, a sound, a mantra. The inward-attention component is the relevant variable.
  • Seek trauma-informed support. If trauma history is relevant, working with a therapist who understands both trauma treatment and contemplative practice is much better than working with either alone.
  • Contact Cheetah House (cheetahhouse.org). They offer free consultations and are the most sophisticated resource in this space.

The honest version of the practice is one that includes this: some people, in some circumstances, are better served by other things. That doesn't indict meditation — it just makes its claims more accurate.
