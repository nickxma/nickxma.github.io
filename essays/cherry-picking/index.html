The Cherry-Picking Problem

You can't be an atheist about every god except one

~18 min read · Interactive

I was trying to explain non-dual meditation to my father — a man whose entire career was built on the kind of ego-driven motivation that mindfulness practitioners are supposed to dissolve. Fear of failure, chip on his shoulder, the need to prove something. And I found myself making an argument I couldn't finish.

The argument was simple enough: you can learn to see that thoughts think themselves. That the sense of being a thinker behind your thoughts is itself just another appearance in consciousness. That what feels like "you" deciding, planning, worrying — all of that is arising on its own, and the "you" who seems to author it is a kind of mirage.

My father listened patiently. Then he asked the question I was hoping he wouldn't: Okay, but does that apply to everything? To the thoughts that make me work hard? To the part of me that wants to build something?

I wanted to say no. I wanted to say you could have this insight about the neurotic thoughts — the self-doubt, the status anxiety, the 3 AM rumination — without it touching the motivated thoughts. The ambition. The drive. The part that gets up early and does the thing.

But I couldn't finish the argument because it doesn't hold. You can't say you want this insight to be true of the thoughts you're neurotic about, but not the thoughts that make you driven. That's like being an atheist about every god except one.

The neurotic self and the motivated self aren't two separate selves. They're the same process of identification, viewed from different angles.

The Move

Here is the pattern, stated plainly: practitioners who want the liberation from the bad parts of ego — insecurity, status anxiety, rumination, the constant low-grade hum of am I enough? — while keeping the good parts. The ambition, the competitive drive, the sustained commitment to hard goals, the fire that makes long projects feel worth finishing.

This is the unspoken assumption behind most "mindfulness and performance" discourse. It is the implicit promise of every meditation app that markets itself alongside productivity tools: you can dissolve identification selectively. You can keep the engine and strip the noise.

Executives meditate to be less reactive in meetings while remaining every bit as driven to hit their numbers. Athletes meditate to dissolve performance anxiety while preserving the competitive edge that produces the anxiety. Writers meditate to quiet the inner critic while hoping the inner ambition survives the surgery.

The move is always the same: take the deconstructive power of the practice, aim it at the parts of the self that hurt, and leave the rest untouched. It sounds reasonable. It sounds, in fact, like exactly what a well-designed intervention should do — target the pathology, spare the function.

The problem is that the pathology and the function are not as separable as the move assumes.

The implicit bargain: dissolve the suffering, keep the striving. Most serious practitioners eventually discover this bargain cannot be honored on its original terms.

The Atheist Analogy

The core argument is structural, not psychological. Once you have genuinely seen that thoughts think themselves — that the self who appears to author your decisions is itself just another appearance, no more substantial than the decisions it seems to be making — you cannot unsee it for the convenient cases.

This is not a matter of discipline or practice technique. It is a matter of logical consistency. The insight into the constructed nature of self applies with equal force to the neurotic self and the motivated self. They are not two selves running on separate hardware. They are the same process of identification, producing different content at different moments.

The anxious thought "What if I fail?" and the ambitious thought "I'm going to make this work" have different emotional valences, different practical implications, different relationships to your wellbeing. But they share an identical deep structure: a self that feels like the author, a future that feels like it depends on that self's performance, and an identification with outcomes that gives the whole thing its force.

To claim that the anxious version of this process is an illusion while the ambitious version is real is exactly like being an atheist about every god except one. The mechanism that makes them feel real is the same mechanism. You cannot debunk it selectively.

The non-dual teaching is explicit about this, if you listen carefully. There is no self, period. Not "there is no anxious self but there is an ambitious self." Not "there is no grasping self but there is a purposeful self." The teaching does not make exceptions for the versions of identification that happen to produce outcomes you like. It points at the process itself and says: look — this is not what it seems to be.

The insight into the constructed nature of self applies with equal force to the neurotic self and the motivated self. You cannot debunk identification selectively.

Consider what it would mean to take the insight seriously but only in certain domains. On Monday, you see through the self that's anxious about the presentation. The anxiety dissolves. You recognize it as content arising in awareness, authorless, impersonal. On Tuesday, you feel a surge of ambition about a new project — the excitement, the self-image of being the person who pulls it off, the drive to outperform. And here you stop looking. Here the self is real again. Here identification is welcomed back because it's producing something you want.

This is not a coherent position. It is a negotiation. And like most negotiations, it works for a while — until the terms become visible.

Thought Experiment The Two Selves Test

Pick two moments from the last week. One where you felt anxious, insecure, or inadequate — the kind of self-referential suffering that meditation is supposed to address. And one where you felt driven, purposeful, or ambitious — the kind of engagement that most people want to preserve.

Now ask: in both cases, was there a sense of being the author of the experience? A "you" who was having the anxiety, and a "you" who was having the ambition?

If you're honest, the answer is yes. The felt sense of selfhood — of being the one to whom this is happening, the one who must respond — is present in both. The content differs. The emotional quality differs. But the underlying architecture is the same: identification with a self-narrative, projected into a future, organized around outcomes.

The non-dual insight, if genuine, sees through that architecture. Not through its worst products. Through the architecture itself.

The Teachers Who See It

The most honest teachers in the contemplative traditions have noticed this problem. They don't always resolve it, but they name it — which is more than most discourse manages.

Joko Beck, the Zen teacher, put it with characteristic bluntness:

"As human beings, we don't want to stay with this at all. The minute you become the sensation, totally surrender to it, you've disappeared. And the whole focus of our life isn't to disappear, but to glorify ourself." — Joko Beck

Beck names the tension without resolving it. The practice asks you to disappear. Your life asks you to persist. These are not compatible projects dressed in compatible language. They are, at a certain depth, opposed. The glorification of self that makes a career, a family, a body of work, a reputation — all of that requires precisely the kind of identification that the practice is designed to dissolve.

You can soften this opposition, negotiate with it, work out temporary truces. But you cannot eliminate it by pretending the practice only targets the bad versions of self-glorification. Ambition is self-glorification. Achievement is self-glorification. The drive to leave something behind, to matter, to be remembered or respected or simply competent — all of it runs on the same fuel the practice is trying to burn.

James Low, a Dzogchen teacher, goes further. He deconstructs even the most selfless-sounding motivation a practitioner might claim:

"In this and all my future lives, may I remember the vows of our Bodhisattva and work for the liberation of all beings — that's very nice. But it's a sequence of words forming in sentences, forming an intention. It's completely dualistic." — James Low

Low's point cuts deeper than it first appears. The bodhisattva vow — the aspiration to liberate all sentient beings from suffering — is generally considered the noblest possible motivation within Buddhism. It is the get-out-of-jail-free card for ego concerns: I'm not doing this for myself, I'm doing it for everyone.

And Low says: no. Even this is dualistic. Even this is a self forming an intention about a future and projecting itself into that future as the agent who will fulfill the vow. The structure of identification is present in the highest aspiration exactly as it is present in the pettiest vanity. If you are serious about non-duality, you cannot exempt your noblest motives from the same scrutiny you apply to your worst ones.

This is not nihilism. Low is not saying the bodhisattva vow is worthless. He is saying it is not the escape from duality that it presents itself as. It is duality wearing better clothes. And if the practice is genuinely about seeing through duality, it has to see through this too.

Why the Community Doesn't Say This

If the cherry-picking problem is this straightforward — if the insight either applies universally or isn't the insight it claims to be — why doesn't the mindfulness community say so?

Adyashanti, one of the most respected teachers in the Western non-dual tradition, gets closer than most. He describes the theoretical possibility of what he calls a "functional ego" — a self that operates without identification, that does the practical work of navigating life without the weight of personal investment:

"We need something to orient us in life, but that orienting principle can actually operate and be empty. Then you just have the functional aspect of the ego without the identity aspect." — Adyashanti

And then, immediately, he says: "Well, good luck living your life that way."

This is remarkable honesty from a teacher who has built a career on the possibility of awakening. He describes the theoretical endpoint — ego without identification, function without investment — and then laughs at it. He knows it doesn't work in practice. He says so. And then the discourse moves on, because there is nowhere comfortable for it to go.

The community cannot say "you can't cherry-pick" because it would collapse the value proposition. People come to meditation to feel better, not to feel less. If you tell practitioners that the insight applies equally to their ambition and their anxiety — that dissolving the neurotic self also loosens the motivated self, because they were never separate — some meaningful fraction of them will leave. And the ones who stay will need a much more complicated conversation than the marketing allows for.

The economic incentives are obvious but worth stating. Meditation apps, retreat centers, teacher trainings, book deals — all of this depends on the implicit promise that you can have the liberation without the loss. That you can deepen your practice indefinitely and only good things happen. That equanimity and excellence are not just compatible but synergistic. That the highest performers are also the most awakened, or could be, if they just practice enough.

This is the spiritual equivalent of a weight-loss product that promises you can eat whatever you want. It is not that the mechanism is fake. It is that the mechanism has costs that the marketing omits.

Thought Experiment The Teacher's Dilemma

Imagine you are a meditation teacher. You have genuine insight. You have also built a career, a following, a livelihood around sharing that insight.

A student comes to you and says: "I've been practicing for five years. My anxiety is gone. But so is my ambition. I used to care about my career, my creative work, my physical training. Now I can sit with anything. And I'm not sure I'm doing anything."

What do you say?

If you're honest, you might say: "Yes, that can happen. The insight doesn't distinguish between the suffering you wanted to dissolve and the drive you wanted to keep. They're the same process." But this answer has consequences. It means your teaching has costs. It means "more practice" is not always the right prescription. It means some people should scale back, or stop, or do something different entirely.

Most teachers don't say this. Not because they're dishonest, but because the framework they operate within doesn't have room for it. "Enough" is not a concept that maps onto "awakening."

Joan Tollifson, a teacher with roots in both Zen and non-dual traditions, concedes the functional point directly:

"We need a biological sense of location to function. We need healthy physical and psychological boundaries… No matter how enlightened we are, unless we have a brain injury, some sense of being a particular person in the play of life inevitably remains." — Joan Tollifson

This is honest. It is also in tension with the broader non-dual project. If some sense of self inevitably remains — if the ego cannot be fully dissolved without a brain injury — then the teaching that there is "no self" is either wrong or describing something that doesn't actually map onto lived experience as cleanly as it claims. The self persists because it has to. The question is what happens when you've spent years trying to dissolve something that cannot, by Tollifson's own admission, actually go away.

What Cherry-Picking Actually Looks Like

In theory, the cherry-picking problem is abstract. In practice, it is the most common thing in the room at any meditation gathering. You can see it everywhere once you know what to look for.

The meditator who can sit with a difficult emotion for forty minutes but still works eighty-hour weeks chasing a promotion. She has not dissolved ego-identification. She has redirected the deconstructive power of her practice toward the emotions that slow her down, while leaving the identification that drives her completely intact. The practice is in service of the ego, not opposed to it.

The practitioner who has "seen through the self" but still checks his follower count. He has had genuine insight — moments where the sense of being a separate self dropped away, where consciousness appeared as it is, without a center. And then the moment passes, and the self reconstitutes, and it reconstitutes with all its ordinary concerns, including the concern about whether people are paying attention to him. The insight was real. The cherry-picking is also real. They coexist.

The teacher who speaks about non-attachment while running a business. She offers retreats on letting go, writes books about the illusion of self, and simultaneously manages a brand, negotiates contracts, monitors her market position relative to other teachers. This is not hypocrisy, exactly. It is the human condition reasserting itself after the meditation bell rings. The insight is available in formal practice. The identification is available everywhere else.

These aren't failures. They're evidence that cherry-picking is what everyone actually does, because the alternative — genuine non-selective insight applied to every domain of life — is functionally unlivable in the world as it is.

The Silicon Valley mindfulness scene is perhaps the purest distillation. Here, meditation is explicitly framed as a performance technology. The goal is not liberation from the self but optimization of the self. Dissolve the anxiety, keep the ambition. Quiet the inner critic, amplify the inner strategist. See through the neurotic patterns, but only the ones that impede execution.

This is cherry-picking stated as a business plan. And it works, to a degree. People genuinely become less reactive, more present in difficult conversations, better at managing stress. These are real benefits. But they are benefits purchased by applying a universal insight in a deliberately non-universal way. The insight says: there is no self. The practitioner says: there is no anxious self. The ambitious self is fine.

Thought Experiment The Selective Meditator

You're at dinner with friends. Someone asks about your meditation practice. You describe the benefits: less reactivity, more presence, the ability to catch spiraling thoughts before they take hold.

Now imagine they ask: "Has it changed your ambition at all? Your drive? Your willingness to sacrifice now for something later?"

If you're honest, the answer is probably complicated. Maybe it has. Maybe you've noticed a softening not just of anxiety but of urgency. Maybe futures that once felt pressing now feel more metabolizable. Maybe you've become not just calmer but also, in some domains, less motivated.

But that's not the answer most practitioners give at dinner. The answer at dinner is the cherry-picked version: I'm calmer but just as driven. I'm more present but just as productive. I've dissolved the noise but kept the signal.

Ask yourself: is that actually true? Or is it the story you tell because the alternative is too complicated to explain over pasta?

The Honest Position

If the insight into the constructed nature of self is real — and I believe it is, not as metaphysics but as something that can be directly experienced — then it applies to everything. To the goals you love, the ambitions you're proud of, the "meaningful" work you do, the relationships you've built your identity around, the self-image of being someone who meditates seriously. All of it is the same process of identification. All of it is the self constructing itself and believing in the construction.

The honest response isn't to pretend you can be selective. It is to acknowledge that most practitioners are, in fact, doing something much messier than the teachings describe: negotiating a partial truce with their ego, dissolving it in the domains where it causes pain and quietly preserving it in the domains where it generates energy.

That negotiation is probably fine. It might even be wise. A human being who has completely dissolved identification with outcomes, goals, relationships, and self-continuity would not look like a serene sage navigating daily life with effortless grace. They would look like someone who has lost the plot. The functional ego exists for reasons. Motivation exists for reasons. The fear of consequences that organizes prudent behavior exists for reasons. Dissolving all of it is not liberation. It is a different kind of impairment.

But here is the thing: that negotiated truce, that partial dissolution, that selective application of a universal insight — it is a different project than "awakening." It is more like therapy with mystical characteristics. It uses contemplative tools to achieve psychological goals. It borrows the language of liberation while pursuing the objectives of optimization. And there is nothing wrong with that, except that calling it awakening creates confusion the community refuses to address.

Most practitioners are negotiating a partial truce with their ego — dissolving it where it hurts and preserving it where it helps. That's probably wise. But it's a different project than awakening.

The non-dual insight either applies universally or it isn't the insight it claims to be. But applying it universally dissolves the agent who would apply it. This is not a paradox to be resolved with a clever reframe. It is a feature of the territory. The map says there is no self. The territory contains a self that needs to navigate using the map. Both statements are true at the same time, and the tension between them cannot be collapsed without losing something real.

The question, then, is not whether cherry-picking is happening. It is. Everywhere, in everyone who meditates and also lives an ordinary life. The question is whether the mindfulness community can be honest about the map it is selling.

Because the map says: no self, no exceptions.

And the practitioners — all of them, including the teachers — are quietly adding exceptions every day. They are keeping the self that loves their children, the self that wants to write the next book, the self that cares about paying rent and staying healthy and being respected by peers. They are dissolving identification in the meditation hall and reconstituting it in the parking lot.

That is not a failure. It is the human condition, working within a framework that does not fully account for it.

Whether the framework should be updated to acknowledge this — or whether the tension is itself the teaching — might be the most important question the contemplative tradition is not asking.
