The Avoidance Problem

~9 min read ·

There's a particular kind of meditator who practices hardest at exactly the wrong moments.

They notice tension in a relationship and sit with it. They feel the low-grade dread of a conversation they've been putting off and bring awareness to it. They sense career stagnation, a gathering dissatisfaction, the quiet signal that something in their life needs to change — and they meditate. Genuinely. Carefully. With real skill.

The tension softens. The dread loosens. The dissatisfaction becomes something they can hold without being held by it. They feel better.

Nothing changes.

This is not a story about failing at mindfulness. It is a story about succeeding at it. That is the problem.

What Emotions Are For

Before mindfulness, a difficult emotion had a trajectory. Anxiety would accumulate until it forced a decision. Dissatisfaction would build until it demanded action. The low-grade dread of an avoided conversation would intensify until having the conversation finally felt less costly than not having it. The discomfort was the mechanism. It was doing something.

Emotions have two functions that are easy to conflate. One is phenomenological: they feel a certain way, and that feeling is often genuinely unpleasant. The other is informational: they are signals. They point at something in the world that hasn't been addressed. A threatened relationship. A misaligned career. A boundary that has been crossed repeatedly and never named.

Mindfulness is extraordinarily effective at the first function. It creates enough distance from a feeling that the feeling stops running you. It interrupts the loop. It reduces a kind of suffering that is, in many cases, genuinely unnecessary. This is real, and it is worth something.

The problem is that it does not distinguish between the phenomenological and the informational. It meets both with the same instruction: be present, notice, don't add resistance. The feeling softens whether it was excessive noise or a critical message.

Once you're good enough at this, you can metabolize the signal before it delivers its payload.

The Mechanism

Here is how it works in practice.

You're in a relationship that has been quietly draining for longer than you've admitted. You know this the way you know most true things about yourself: vaguely, at the edge, not yet in focus. The unease shows up at odd moments — after a particular conversation, in the quality of an evening, in a mood you can't quite source.

You sit with it. You notice the sensation without elaborating it into a story. You don't catastrophize. You don't add commentary. You return to the breath. The feeling, which would otherwise build toward a confrontation or a decision, softens and disperses.

Tomorrow it will be back. And you will sit with it again.

The practice is working exactly as intended. You are being with a difficult emotion rather than being swept away by it. What you are not doing is following the emotion to what it points at. You have met it skillfully — which is to say, you have deflected it skillfully.

The conversation doesn't happen. The decision doesn't get made. The situation stays the same, and you stay a little more equanimous about it.

This is what one of the most experienced teachers in the non-dual tradition meant when he acknowledged, with something close to dry alarm, that if you get good at meditating, you can get really good at hiding from yourself. The practice that was designed to make you confront yourself becomes the tool you reach for to avoid the confrontation. Not through distraction or numbness or the usual forms of running away. Through a more refined form of the same thing.

You might want to re-look at how you're going about it, he said. Maybe you've actually gotten so good at it that you no longer confront much of anything.

That's exactly right. And it is very hard to see because the alternative — avoidance through distraction, rumination, reactivity, projection — looks so much worse.

The Sophistication Problem

Most avoidance is recognizable. You know when you're scrolling instead of working. You know when you're picking a fight instead of having the real conversation. You know when you're busy instead of present.

Mindfulness-as-avoidance doesn't feel like avoidance. It feels like maturity. You're not running from your feelings; you're sitting with them. You're not projecting; you're observing. You're not numbing out; you're fully present with the discomfort. You've read the books. You know what spiritual bypassing is. You would never do that.

And yet the job the emotion was trying to do still doesn't get done.

This is the problem that makes this version of avoidance so durable. It passes all the surface tests. You're not white-knuckling through difficult feelings. You're not suppressing. You're not dissociating. By every visible metric, you're doing the practice correctly. The only thing you're not doing is asking what the feeling was trying to tell you — and whether you've actually answered that question, or only quieted the asking of it.

There is a meaningful difference between processing an emotion and being present with it. Processing eventually resolves into something: a decision, a conversation, a changed behavior, a genuine letting go after something has actually been addressed. Being present with it can loop indefinitely. You can be present with the same feeling for years.

The skill that lets you sit with discomfort without being consumed by it is genuinely useful. The same skill can be pointed at a feeling and used to prevent it from ever ripening into action.

What This Looks Like From the Inside

The internal experience is not dramatic.

It doesn't feel like avoidance. It feels like groundedness. You're the calm one in the situation. You don't react. You observe your reactions and choose your responses. This is, depending on the context, either wisdom or a way of keeping everything at a managed distance.

The difference is hard to locate from inside the practice. What you feel is usually something like: I'm okay with this. What you can't always feel is whether "okay with this" means you've genuinely arrived at peace with an unchangeable situation, or whether you've found a way to be okay with a situation you could change but haven't decided to.

There is a version of equanimity that is the result of genuine clarity — you've looked at the situation honestly, understood what you can and cannot do, and released what you needed to release. And there is a version of equanimity that is what you get when you've become skilled enough to metabolize the charge from a feeling before asking what it was about.

Both feel similar from the inside. The first is hard-won and points at something real. The second is a comfortable place that is very easy to live in indefinitely.

The Honest Corrective

This is not an argument against mindfulness practice. It's a narrower point about one of its failure modes.

The failure mode is not that mindfulness makes you passive. You can be extremely active and extremely good at using mindfulness to avoid what matters. The failure mode is subtler: you can use the skill of present-moment awareness to prevent a feeling from ever doing the thing feelings are sometimes supposed to do — accumulate enough force to push you toward a change you know you need to make.

The useful question isn't can you be with this feeling? You already know you can. The question is: what is this feeling trying to get you to do, and have you done it?

Not every difficult feeling is trying to get you to do something. Some are just noise — recursive worry about a future that hasn't happened, self-criticism that isn't useful, fear that isn't tracking any real threat. For those, meeting them with non-reactive awareness and letting them dissolve is exactly right.

But some feelings are carrying information. A relationship problem that hasn't been addressed. A work situation that has been tolerated past the point where tolerating it makes sense. A truth about yourself that you've been circling without landing on. For those, the relevant question is not how to be with the feeling but what the feeling is pointing at.

The difference between these two types is not obvious, and that is part of the problem. The practice doesn't make the distinction for you. It offers the same instruction regardless: notice, don't add resistance, return. That instruction is correct in both cases, but in the second case it's incomplete. Being present with a signal is not the same as receiving what the signal is trying to deliver.

The correction, if there is one, is less a technique than a habit of inquiry: after the feeling softens, before you conclude that you've dealt with it — has anything actually changed? Is there something this feeling was pointing at that you've now addressed, or something you've learned to be more comfortable not addressing?

The question is small. What it can surface is not.

There are people who meditate with great commitment and change very little. Not because the practice isn't working. Because it's working well enough to make the status quo tolerable, and tolerable is a long way from honest.

The practice can make you calmer in a situation that should make you uncomfortable. It can make you patient with something that deserves impatience. It can make you accepting of a circumstance that should feel unacceptable.

That is not equanimity. It is equanimity's shadow — a peace that is purchased by meeting every difficult feeling just skillfully enough that you never have to ask what it was trying to show you.

The sophistication is the point. This kind of avoidance doesn't announce itself. It looks like the work. It has the posture of practice. And the longer you do it, the harder it gets to distinguish from the real thing.
