Should You Get Into Mindfulness?

The case for treating meditation like medicine, not vitamins

My father takes a GLP-1 medication for weight loss. Every month his doctor adjusts the dosage — up or down, depending on how he's responding. Eventually, the doctor might tell him he doesn't need it anymore.

I've been meditating seriously for years and no one — not a teacher, not an app, not a single voice in the growing clinical literature — has ever suggested I might have had enough.

One of the few researchers studying meditation's adverse effects puts it simply: all practices are beneficial for some people, some of the time. That qualifier changes everything. Medicine has dosage. Physical training has enough. Mindfulness, somehow, does not.

It's almost like being given Parkinson's medication when you came in for weight loss. The treatment is real. The question is whether it matches the condition.

All practices are beneficial for some people, some of the time. That qualifier changes everything.

The Default Sales Pitch

Here is how mindfulness is typically sold. It reduces stress. It improves focus. It helps with emotional regulation. It makes you a better partner, parent, colleague. It's good for your blood pressure, your sleep, your immune system. It's compatible with any religion or none. There are no known side effects. More is better.

Most of this is roughly true, at least for some populations at some doses. The early evidence base for mindfulness-based stress reduction is real. The effects on rumination and anxiety are among the more robust findings in the contemplative science literature. I don't want to argue with that.

What I want to argue with is the framing. Because the framing is that of a vitamin — something universally beneficial, with no upper bound, no contraindications, and no conception of "enough." You take it forever. You take more when you can. The only risk is not taking it.

This is not how we talk about anything else that changes the brain.

The vitamin framing is the problem. Not the practice itself. Vitamins don't need dosage. Medicine does. The question is which model better fits a practice that systematically alters how you relate to your own emotions, identity, and sense of future.

Where It Genuinely Works

The strongest case for mindfulness is also the most specific. If you are someone whose suffering is primarily generated by your own mental habits — chronic rumination, over-identification with outcomes, anxiety that spirals rather than informs, a constant narrating self that adds suffering on top of pain — then the shift mindfulness produces is often genuinely helpful.

The mechanism is fairly well understood. You learn to notice thoughts as thoughts rather than as reality. You develop some distance from the narrator. Reactivity slows down. The space between stimulus and response — the famous Viktor Frankl gap, though he may never have said it quite that way — gets wider. For someone trapped in recursive self-criticism or future-catastrophizing, this is real relief. Not metaphorical. Not placebo. The kind of relief that changes the texture of daily life.

If narrative force is currently impairing your functioning — if you are suffering more from your relationship to experience than from experience itself — mindfulness is probably one of the best interventions available. The evidence supports this. The lived experience of millions of people supports this. I support this.

The question is what happens next.

Because the practice doesn't come with a tapering schedule. It doesn't say: your rumination has improved, your reactivity is down, you can maintain with twenty minutes a day now, and here's what to watch for if you're overdoing it. It says: go deeper. There is always more to see. The journey has no destination — or, in some traditions, the destination is so radical that anything short of it represents incomplete understanding.

That framing works beautifully for the people who need more. It works less well for the people who might need less.

The Part Nobody Mentions

"If you get good at meditating, you can really get good at hiding from yourself. It's kind of one of the dangers of meditation, right?" — Adyashanti

Adyashanti is not a critic of meditation. He is one of its most respected Western teachers. And he is saying something that the mainstream discourse almost never says: the practice can function as avoidance.

Not for everyone. Not at every stage. But for some people, at some point, the capacity to meet difficult emotions skillfully becomes the capacity to not have to deal with them at all. The meditation replaces the action the emotion was asking for.

Here is what this looks like in practice. You feel dissatisfied with your career. The dissatisfaction arises as a contraction, a tightness, a thought-loop about wasted potential. In one mode, that discomfort is information. It's unpleasant but it's pointing at something real: you need to make a change. In another mode — the one that serious practice makes increasingly available — you notice the contraction, you soften around it, you return to the present moment. The charge drops. You feel better. Nothing changes.

The second mode is genuinely useful when the emotion is disproportionate to the situation. If you're spiraling about a minor career setback, being able to step back from the narrative is a gift. But what about when the emotion is proportionate? What about when the dissatisfaction is actually telling you the truth — that you are, in fact, in the wrong place, doing the wrong work, and the discomfort is the signal that should drive reorganization?

The problem is that from the inside, these two situations feel identical. Both involve the same gesture: notice, soften, return. What's different is only what the emotion was pointing at. And that's exactly what gets lost in the dissolving.

Thought Experiment The Relationship Signal

You've been feeling a low-grade unhappiness in a relationship. Not crisis-level. Not abuse. Just a persistent sense that something isn't right — that you're performing contentment rather than feeling it.

In one mode, this feeling accumulates until it becomes undeniable. It pushes you toward a difficult conversation, a renegotiation of terms, or an honest admission that the relationship isn't working. The discomfort is the engine of change.

In another mode, you notice the feeling each time it arises, acknowledge it without judgment, and let it pass. You don't suppress it — that would be old-fashioned repression. You meet it. Skillfully. Compassionately. And it dissolves, each time, without ever accumulating into the force that would make you act.

The question: is the second mode wisdom or is it a very sophisticated form of staying stuck?

The Ego Problem

In most Buddhist-derived frameworks, the ego is the problem. It's the source of suffering, the generator of craving and aversion, the illusion that keeps you trapped in samsara. The goal, stated or implied, is to see through it — to recognize that the self you've been defending, promoting, and protecting was never really there in the way you thought.

This framing has a long history and a certain elegance. But it has a practical problem that not enough teachers acknowledge.

"I sometimes ponder how it is that within the religious and spiritual traditions that the ego gets such a bad name." — Diane Musho Hamilton

Hamilton is a Zen teacher with training in developmental psychology, and her alternative framing is worth taking seriously. She prefers to work with a model of outgrowing the ego rather than destroying it. In her view, the ego is not a disease to be cured but a developmental stage to be built through. You need a strong ego before you can meaningfully transcend one.

The distinction matters practically. If you haven't yet built a clear identity — if you don't have a stable sense of who you are, what you value, what you're willing to fight for, and what you're willing to endure — then a practice designed to dissolve the sense of self isn't liberation. It's structural damage. You're demolishing a building that was never finished.

This is the population that the mindfulness mainstream rarely acknowledges exists. Not the anxious overachievers who need to learn to let go. The undermotivated, the directionless, the people whose problem isn't too much ego but not enough. For them, the instruction to "let go of attachment to outcomes" can function as spiritual permission to not try. The teaching meets them exactly where they don't need to be met.

"We need a biological sense of location to function. We need healthy physical and psychological boundaries… No matter how enlightened we are, unless we have a brain injury, some sense of being a particular person in the play of life inevitably remains." — Joan Tollifson

Tollifson's point is deceptively simple. The self isn't optional. It's not a luxury that enlightened people learn to do without. It's the operating system. And there's a meaningful difference between upgrading that operating system and wiping the drive.

Thought Experiment The Unbuilt Ego

Imagine two people at a meditation retreat. The first has spent twenty years building a career, raising children, navigating difficult relationships, and developing a clear sense of what they value. They're wound tight. They carry tension in their shoulders, anxiety about the future, and a relentless internal critic. For them, the instruction to relax, to let go, to stop identifying with the narrator — this is medicine for an actual condition.

The second person is twenty-three. They don't know what they want to do. They haven't committed to a career, a relationship, or a set of values. They experience a vague dissatisfaction that they haven't yet learned to interpret as a signal to build something. They arrive at the retreat looking for direction.

Both receive the same teaching: let go of the self. Notice how identity is constructed. See through the illusion of a fixed, separate you.

For the first person, this might be exactly right. For the second, it might be the worst possible advice — spiritual validation for an incomplete developmental project. Not because the insight is wrong, but because it's being administered to someone who hasn't built the thing the insight is about.

The Britton Data

Willoughby Britton runs the Clinical and Affective Neuroscience Laboratory at Brown University. She is one of the very few researchers who has systematically studied meditation-related adverse effects — not as anecdotes or cautionary tales, but as data.

Her findings are not what the mindfulness industry wants to hear.

"There are people that are reporting that they've lost all emotions altogether and they don't feel pleasure anymore… a woman who no longer felt affection for her children." — Willoughby Britton

This is not a rare edge case from a marginal study. This is a recurring pattern across hundreds of practitioners interviewed by Britton's research team. The most common reports: emotional blunting — not depression exactly, but a flattening of the entire emotional range such that positive and negative experiences both lose their color. Decreased motivation. A sense of depersonalization that practitioners initially interpret as spiritual progress but that, over time, starts to feel more like disconnection than liberation.

"You could have so much equanimity and diminished affect and diminished emotions." — Jared Lindahl

Lindahl, Britton's collaborator, names the mechanism precisely. Equanimity — the thing the practice is supposed to cultivate — can overshoot. The non-monotonic curve that medicine takes for granted applies here too: the dose that helps at one level harms at another. Detachment is useful until it becomes dissociation. Equanimity is beneficial until it becomes flattening. The line between "I'm less reactive" and "I can't feel things anymore" is not as bright as the marketing suggests.

What makes Britton's work particularly important is what it reveals about the community's response — or lack of one. The mindfulness world has no adverse event reporting system. There is no equivalent of the FDA's post-market surveillance. Teachers are not trained to screen for contraindications, to recognize when practice is causing harm, or to suggest that someone reduce their dose. The "already perfect" framework — the idea that awareness is always beneficial and that any difficulty in practice represents a stage to push through — has no conceptual room for "you've had enough."

70% of documented difficulties emerged during intensive retreat. But 30% emerged from daily practice alone — even less than an hour per day. These aren't extreme cases. They're ordinary practitioners doing ordinary amounts of practice.

A Better Question Than "Should I Meditate?"

The question should not be "should I meditate?" any more than the question should be "should I take medication?" The answer depends entirely on the condition.

If you are chronically anxious — if your default state involves recursive worry, over-preparation, difficulty sleeping because your mind won't stop narrating worst cases — then yes, probably. Mindfulness has one of its strongest evidence bases for exactly this condition. The ability to step back from the thought-stream, to see thoughts as events rather than truths, can genuinely change the quality of a life that is being degraded by excessive mental activity.

If you are over-reactive — if small provocations trigger disproportionate responses, if you find yourself in patterns of anger, defensiveness, or emotional flooding that damage your relationships and your judgment — then yes, probably. The capacity to notice a reaction before it reaches full expression, to create even a small gap between trigger and response, is one of the most practically useful things any practice can offer.

If you are over-identified with outcomes — if your sense of self-worth is so tightly coupled to external results that every setback feels existential, every failure feels like proof of fundamental inadequacy — then yes, probably. The insight that you are not your performance, not your productivity, not the sum of your accomplishments, can be genuinely freeing in exactly the way the teachers describe.

But.

If you are already pretty calm — if your problem isn't excess anxiety but insufficient urgency, not too much self-criticism but too little self-demand — then the calculus changes. More equanimity, for this person, doesn't solve a problem. It deepens a condition that's already present: a comfortable relationship with the status quo that doesn't need further softening.

If you are undermotivated — if you're struggling to commit to long-term goals, to tolerate the discomfort of sustained effort, to care enough about future outcomes to organize your present around them — then the last thing you might need is a practice whose central instruction is to return to the present moment and notice that it's already fine.

If your problem is not that you care too much but that you care too little, mindfulness may not be the right treatment. Not because it doesn't work. Because it works on a condition you don't have.

Thought Experiment The Differential Diagnosis

A doctor doesn't prescribe blood pressure medication without measuring blood pressure. They don't prescribe antidepressants because "everyone could use a mood boost." They diagnose first. They match the treatment to the condition. And they have a concept of when the treatment has done its work.

Now imagine a doctor who prescribed the same medication to every patient who walked in. Anxious? Take this. Calm? Take this. Motivated? Take this. Unmotivated? Take this. The medication genuinely helps a large subset of patients. But because the doctor has no diagnostic framework — no way to distinguish who needs it from who doesn't — they give it to everyone and interpret all outcomes as confirmation that it works.

That is roughly the current state of the mindfulness industry. The treatment is real. The diagnostic framework is missing. And the business model is structured around indefinite use.

No Exit Strategy

Here is the asymmetry that no one in the mindfulness world addresses. The community has sophisticated, beautiful, well-tested language for why you should start. It has a rich tradition of encouragement for going deeper. It has frameworks for understanding difficulty as breakthrough, plateau as resistance, doubt as ego defending itself.

What it does not have — at all — is language for "you've had enough."

No teacher says: your anxiety has improved to within normal range, your reactivity is manageable, you have a functional relationship with your own thoughts, and at this point more practice may be doing more than you need it to. No app sends a notification that says: based on your patterns, you might benefit from doing less of this. No retreat center suggests that this might be your last one for a while.

The absence is not an oversight. It is structural. The framework of awakening only points in one direction. There is always more to realize. Awareness can always deepen. The self can always be seen through more thoroughly. In this frame, the idea of "enough" is itself a sign of insufficient understanding — a subtle form of the ego defending its territory.

Which means that the framework is self-sealing. Any suggestion that you might have had enough is interpretable, within the framework, as evidence that you haven't. Any sense that the practice might be doing something you don't want is treatable, within the framework, as resistance to be sat with. The only direction the system allows is forward.

Medicine has a different word for a treatment that you can never stop taking, that interprets any desire to stop as a symptom of the condition it treats, and that has no concept of a completed course.

Whether that makes mindfulness a path or a trap depends on where you're starting from.

Is This For You?

Four questions to help you think about whether mindfulness matches your current condition. Not a diagnosis — a starting point for honest reflection.

1. What is your default relationship with your own thoughts?

2. When you think about long-term goals — career, health, relationships — what do you feel?

3. How do you typically respond to discomfort — physical or emotional?

4. If someone you trusted told you "you should try meditating less," how would that land?
